Project Overview

This project combines historical research and digital humanities methods to examine Irish–Indigenous interactions through the intersecting lenses of colonialism, displacement, solidarity, and memory. It treats history not as a linear narrative, but as a network of overlapping relationships shaped by empire and resistance.

Research Questions

  • How did the Choctaw donation of 1847 shape transatlantic Irish–Indigenous relationships?
  • Where do Irish and Indigenous histories intersect through colonial systems of displacement and governance?
  • How are these interactions remembered, commemorated, or reframed in the present?
  • How can digital tools reshape how we visualize and interpret colonial history?

Methodology

This project draws on archival documents, secondary scholarship, historical maps, and institutional records to construct a layered interpretation of Irish–Indigenous interactions. These sources form the evidentiary basis for understanding colonial entanglements across the Atlantic world.

However, archival records are often incomplete and reflect colonial perspectives. To address this limitation, the project is informed by Indigenous methodologies that prioritize relational knowledge, oral histories, and community memory. These approaches challenge the assumption that written archives are the only legitimate form of historical evidence.

Digital Humanities Approach

As a digital history project, this site uses interactive mapping (Leaflet), timeline visualization, and a structured event database to represent historical relationships across time and space. These tools allow users to explore patterns that are difficult to perceive in traditional narrative formats.

Interpretive Framework

This project requires careful attention to the complexity of identity under colonialism. Irish migrants occupied an ambivalent position as both subjects of British imperial rule and participants in settler colonial structures abroad. The analysis therefore resists simple binaries in favor of contextual interpretation.

Why This Work Matters

By combining historical research with digital visualization, this project challenges simplified narratives and foregrounds interconnected histories. It aims to produce a more nuanced and ethically grounded understanding of Irish–Indigenous relationships.

User Profile

This project is designed for readers interested in colonial history, transnational Indigenous studies, and digital storytelling. It assumes a user who benefits from visual navigation, guided interpretation, and layered contextual information rather than dense archival analysis alone.

Design & Structure

The website is structured as a narrative-driven research environment. The homepage introduces core themes, while the timeline, map, and database function as interconnected analytical tools that allow users to move between spatial, chronological, and thematic perspectives.

Challenges & Limitations

  • Balancing narrative accessibility with historical complexity
  • Avoiding oversimplification of colonial and Indigenous relationships
  • Managing uneven availability of primary source material
  • Integrating multiple digital tools into a cohesive interpretive system
